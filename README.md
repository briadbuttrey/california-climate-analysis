# California Climate Zones Research Summary

## Overview
California's climate zones are diverse due to its varied topography and geography. According to Wikipedia's *Climate of California* page, the state experiences several Köppen climate classifications:

- **Mediterranean climate (Csb, Csa)**: Dominant along the coast, including Los Angeles and San Francisco. Characterized by mild, wet winters and warm, dry summers. Coastal areas like the Bay Area have cooler summers due to fog.
- **Semi‑arid (BSk)**: Inland valleys and foothills, with hot summers and cooler winters.
- **Desert (BWh, BWk)**: Southeastern regions like the Mojave and Colorado Deserts, extremely hot and dry.
- **Alpine (Dfb, ET)**: High Sierra Nevada mountains with cold, snowy winters.

## 2024 Weather Patterns
Recent patterns (2024 data) show influences from climate change, including prolonged droughts, heatwaves, and increased wildfire risk. Key observations:

- **Coastal Mediterranean**: Mild temperatures; Los Angeles averaged 68 °F summer highs, San Francisco cooler at 62 °F due to marine layer.
- **Central Valley Semi‑arid**: Extreme heat; 2024 highs exceeded 110 °F in July, exacerbating wildfires.
- **Deserts**: Record heat in Death Valley (130 °F+), minimal rain (<2 in annually).
- **Mountains**: Heavy Sierra snowpack early 2024 from El Niño, aiding water reserves but causing floods.

The 2024 season featured prolonged heat domes, intensified wildfires (e.g., Park Fire), and shifting precipitation. Microclimates amplify differences—coastal fog can cool cities like San Francisco by 10–20 °F versus inland areas. Climate change projections indicate drier futures.
